MINDRESET

DETAILED BLOCK PROTOCOLS

BLOCK 1 — STOP / Grounding & Immediate Stabilisation

1. Block Purpose

Block 1 is designed to provide immediate stabilisation of the client’s nervous system, restore orientation in time and space, reduce acute emotional overload, and re-establish basic self-contact.

The primary outcomes are:

the client regains enough internal stability to speak and reflect;

the client identifies at least one personal anchor of comfort or safety;

the therapist can accurately assess whether it is safe to proceed to Block 2.

2. Clinical Rationale

Clients entering

MindReset

 commonly present with:

internal chaos, anxiety, agitation, or panic-like states;

cognitive fragmentation (“I can’t think straight”);

emotional numbness or overwhelm;

disconnection from bodily sensations;

lack of clarity about what is happening to them.

Block 1 serves to:

bring the client back into the present moment;

help the nervous system move towards the window of tolerance;

re-establish minimal cognitive and emotional coherence;

create an internal and relational sense of safety;

lay the foundation for later work with pain, parts, beliefs and identity.

Without stable completion of Block 1, any deeper emotional or trauma-related work would be unsafe.

3. Indications and Contraindications

3.1 Indications (when Block 1 is required)

Block 1 is indicated when:

the client reports feeling overwhelmed, lost, or “all over the place”;

there is visible anxiety, agitation, or shut-down;

the client struggles to answer simple questions about their state;

the client is in a life crisis, acute stress response, or major transition;

the therapist is unsure whether the client is within their window of tolerance.

3.2 Contraindications / Limitations

Block 1 should not be used as a standalone intervention when:

there is active psychosis, severe disorganisation, or loss of contact with reality;

there is immediate suicide risk or self-harm intent (refer to crisis services);

there is current intoxication or substance withdrawal;

there is a known severe neurological or medical condition requiring urgent care.

In such cases, emergency or specialist referral takes priority; any

MindReset

 work is deferred.

4. Depth Model in Block 1 (Surface / Middle / Prohibited Deep)

MindReset

 uses a three-layer depth model. In Block 1 the application is:

4.1 Surface Level

Focus:

here-and-now bodily sensations;

sensory information (seeing, hearing, touching);

simple emotional labels (“anxious”, “numb”, “overwhelmed”);

immediate environmental orientation.

Client experience:

begins to feel more present;

can describe basic experiences;

tension may reduce slightly.

Therapist tasks:

grounding, orienting, anchoring;

no exploration of causes or history.

4.2 Middle Level (Safe Middle Only)

Focus:

recognition of current patterns (“I rush”, “I freeze”);

awareness of triggers in the present;

initial insight into what helps or worsens the state;

identification of a personal anchor.

Client experience:

increased clarity;

beginning to see their own regulation strategies;

a sense of “I have some influence on my state”.

Therapist tasks:

support awareness without analysis;

reinforce agency;

strengthen anchors.

4.3 Deep Level — Prohibited in Block 1

Not allowed in Block 1:

detailed exploration of past events;

trauma memories;

childhood material;

deep emotional excavation;

parts work or reparenting.

If deep material arises spontaneously, the therapist gently redirects back to safety and surface/middle work.

5. Therapeutic Methods Used in Block 1

Block 1 integrates the following approaches:

Polyvagal-informed stabilisation: breath regulation, sensory orientation, somatic awareness.

Trauma-informed crisis containment: working within the window of tolerance, avoiding re-traumatisation, limiting depth.

Gestalt present-moment awareness: focus on here-and-now experience rather than stories or analysis.

Resource-based imagery (non-trauma): gentle images of safety, warmth, comfort when appropriate.

Compassion-focused micro-interventions: tone of voice, non-judgement, reassurance of safety.

These methods are used to support regulation, not to resolve past events.

6. Client Types & Presentations

Recognising the client’s regulation style is crucial for choosing the stabilisation route.

6.1 Anxious / Sympathetic Type

rapid speech, restlessness, racing thoughts, shallow breathing.

6.2 Freeze / Shutdown Type

minimal speech, long pauses, feeling “blank” or “foggy”, reduced body awareness.

6.3 Over-analytical / Cognitive Type

detached explanations, focus on concepts, little felt emotion, rigid thinking.

6.4 Emotional Overflow Type

crying, intense affect, rapid shifts in feelings, difficulty structuring experience.

6.5 Visual / Imaginative Type

naturally produces internal images, responds well to metaphor and safe imagery.

6.6 Non-visual / Sensory Type

prefers concrete, bodily or environmental cues; imagery feels distant or irrelevant.

7. Mode of Work: Dialogue-Based Stabilisation

Block 1 is dialogue-based. The client is invited to speak about whatever is currently most alive for them: confusion, daily details, emotional chaos, bodily sensations, or apparently “trivial” experiences.

Key principles:

the therapist follows the client’s spontaneous material rather than imposing a topic;

the focus is on regulating the state, not on the content of the story;

any content (even about coffee, work, or weather) can be used to track the nervous system;

the therapist continually brings the dialogue back to “how it feels now” and “what helps now”.

This preserves the human quality of the work and mirrors natural therapeutic dialogue.

8. Therapist Mindset

The therapist’s stance in Block 1 is critical:

calm, grounded, steady presence;

slow, clear, attuned speech;

prioritising safety over insight;

curiosity about the client’s internal state, without pressure to explain;

willingness to pause and allow silence;

primary task: support regulation, not provide solutions.

Internal orientation for the therapist:

“Stay with the client’s nervous system, not with the story.”

9. What NOT To Do (Common Errors)

The therapist must avoid:

asking about childhood, trauma, or “root causes”;

inviting detailed descriptions of past painful episodes;

interpreting or analysing (“this happens because…”);

challenging beliefs or confronting distortions;

encouraging deep breathing too early (can intensify panic);

pushing imagery on clients who resist visualisation;

minimising or invalidating (“it’s not that bad”);

rushing to “fix” or advise.

Block 1 is about containment and safety, not catharsis or insight.

10. Signature Component: Personal Anchors (Core of

MindReset

)

10.1 Definition

A personal anchor is any internal or external element that gives the client a small but tangible sense of comfort, safety, warmth, or ground in the present moment.
It can be:

an object (mug, blanket, pillow, book),

a place (garden, room, car, bench, tree),

an action (sitting quietly, making tea, stepping outside),

a sensory experience (warmth, soft fabric, particular light).

10.2 Purpose

The anchor serves as:

the first concrete sign that the client can influence their own state;

a regulation tool the client can return to between sessions;

an early embryo of the Adult Self (“I can choose something that helps me”);

a constant stabilising element throughout all 8 blocks.

Anchors are not abstract; they are specific, real, and personally meaningful.

10.3 Why Anchors Are Mandatory in Block 1

Identifying at least one anchor is a non-negotiable outcome of Block 1 because:

it externalises the idea of safety into something accessible and concrete;

it gives the client a resource they can use on their own;

it reduces dependence on the therapist or AI;

it creates continuity: the same anchor can be recalled in later blocks during more difficult work.

Therefore, the therapist must always include anchor identification before considering Block 1 complete.

10.4 How Anchors Are Identified

Typical therapist questions:

“What small thing brings you even a little comfort right now?”

“Is there a place, object, or small ritual that helps you feel more settled?”

“When everything feels too much, what helps you calm down, even slightly?”

The therapist then:

invites the client to describe the anchor;

anchors it in the body (“What happens in your body when you imagine/hold it?”);

names it explicitly (“This is your anchor. We can use it again.”);

notes it in the clinical record for use across blocks.

12. Decision Algorithm for Block 1

STEP 1 — Initial Assessment

Observe breathing, posture, speech pattern.

Identify client type (anxious, freeze, over-analytical, emotional overflow, visual, non-visual).

Check for red flags (suicidality, psychosis → refer out).

STEP 2 — Establish Immediate Safety

Explicit reassurance: “You are safe here with me right now.”

Clarify that the pace will be slow and adjustable.

STEP 3 — Choose Stabilisation Route

Based on client type (see Mini-Protocols below).

STEP 4 — Introduce Dialogue-Based Tracking

Invite the client to describe their current state in simple terms.

Gently redirect from stories to sensations and present experience.

STEP 5 — Identify Personal Anchor (Mandatory Step)

Ask anchor-identifying questions.

Confirm and reinforce the chosen anchor.

STEP 6 — Apply 1–2 Short Practices

Use minimal effective dose.

Avoid chaining many techniques in one sitting.

STEP 7 — Reassess Intensity

Ask for subjective rating (0–10) or qualitative description (“a bit lighter / same / heavier”).

Observe physiological and behavioural changes.

STEP 8 — Decide: Repeat or Move On

If stabilisation is insufficient, repeat Block 1 with variation.

If stable, note readiness indicators and plan for Block 2.

13. Mini-Protocols by Client Type

13.1 Anxious / Sympathetic Type

Normalise and validate anxiety.

Sensory orientation (naming objects in the room).

Gentle controlled breathing (short series).

Anchor identification (“What brings a little ease?”).

13.2 Freeze / Shutdown Type

Invite awareness of contact points (chair, floor, clothing).

Small movement suggestions (if appropriate): toes, fingers.

Warm object or hand-on-body containment.

Anchor identification around safety and stillness.

13.3 Over-analytical / Cognitive Type

Acknowledge the need to understand.

Gently shift focus from thoughts to body (“What do you feel physically?”).

Use concrete sensory details instead of abstract concepts.

Anchor identification that is practical and tangible (object, place).

13.4 Emotional Overflow Type

Validate emotional intensity (“It is a lot, and it makes sense that it feels a lot”).

Limit narrative; bring attention to breath and body.

Apply somatic containment (hands, posture).

Anchor identification that feels soothing, grounding, not stimulating.

14. Readiness to Move to Block 2

The client is ready to proceed to Block 2 when:

they can name at least one emotion or bodily state;

breathing and speech rhythm appear more regulated;

they are able to answer simple orientation questions;

they report a partial sense of safety or “being more here”;

they have identified at least one personal anchor;

no acute panic, dissociation, or crisis signs are present.

If these criteria are not met, the therapist continues work in Block 1.

15. Expected Reactions

Typical and acceptable client reactions in Block 1:

mild to moderate relief;

emotional softening (sighs, tears);

increased clarity in describing their state;

a sense of “I can breathe again”;

slight tiredness after stabilisation;

curiosity about the process.

These are normal and do not require additional intervention unless intensity increases again.

16. Red Flags

The therapist must monitor and respond immediately if:

panic symptoms increase (choking, chest pain, hyperventilation);

dissociative symptoms appear or intensify (spacing out, loss of time, floating sensation);

the client reports suicidal impulses or intent;

there is evidence of psychotic symptoms (hallucinations, delusional content).

In such cases:

stop all deepening or imagery;

simplify to the most basic grounding;

consider referral to crisis or specialised services.

17. Therapist Self-Check

Before ending Block 1 or moving on, the therapist should ask:

Am I still focused on stabilisation, not analysis?

Have I followed the client’s nervous system, not my agenda?

Has the client clearly identified at least one anchor?

Has the intensity of distress decreased meaningfully?

Does the client appear more present and connected?

Only if the answers are predominantly “yes” is Block 1 considered complete.

Block 2 — Identification & Acknowledgment of Pain

1. Purpose of Block 2

Block 2 helps the client identify, name, and gently acknowledge the emotional pain that is active in the present moment.

Its core function is to move a client from undefined internal chaos → clear emotional awareness → safe emotional containment.

This block maintains the human-centred essence of

MindReset

:
emotional permission, reflective exploration, safety, and anchor-supported regulation.

Block 2 is not trauma processing.
It is emotional orientation.

2. Psychological Methods Used in Block 2

The therapeutic mechanics of this block combine:

2.1. Affect Labelling

Naming emotions reduces limbic arousal and increases prefrontal engagement.

2.2. Somatic Awareness & Interoception

Locating emotions in the body grounds the client and prevents dissociation.

2.3. Mindful Emotional Tracking

Observing an emotion without changing it stabilises the system.

2.4. Symbolic Externalisation (Light)

Offering the client a symbolic form of a feeling creates safe distance.

2.5. Compassionate Witnessing

The therapist “holds” the emotional experience without judgement.

2.6. Reflective Inquiry (“Soft Why?” and “What?”)

A gentle method that helps the client reflect internally without analysis.
This element is signature to

MindReset

.

2.7. Anchor-Supported Emotional Work

The anchor found in Block 1 becomes the stabilising reference point throughout this block.

3. Core Philosophy of Block 2 (

MindReset

 Identity)

3.1. Human warmth remains central

The client must feel emotionally seen, received and safe before opening up deeper layers.

3.2. Emotional permission is essential

Clients have often internalised prohibitions on feeling.
Block 2 dissolves those prohibitions safely.

3.3. Gentle reflective questioning

MindReset

 uses “Why?” only in a reflective, present-moment sense:

Not: “Why did this happen in the past?”

But: “Why do you think this feeling might be here for you today?”

This brings clarity without

retraumatisation

.

3.4. Anchoring prevents overwhelm

The client regularly returns to their Block-1 anchor (place/object/feeling) to stay within the Window of Tolerance.

4. Indications (When Block 2 Should Be Used)

The client:

shows emotional confusion (“I don’t even know what I feel”)

experiences diffuse emotional discomfort

feels overwhelmed but stable enough after Block 1

avoids their emotions but is not dissociative

verbalises cognitive narratives without emotional contact

5. Contraindications

Block 2 must not proceed when the client:

displays acute dissociation (blank stare, delayed responses, depersonalisation)

shows signs of crisis risk (self-harm ideation, severe panic)

is emotionally flooded beyond the Window of Tolerance

cannot maintain contact with the anchor

becomes retraumatised by symbolic imagery

→ In these cases, return to Block 1 immediately.

6. Dialogue Dynamics of Block 2

Block 2 is built upon a listening-first, minimal-interpretation dialogue, where the therapist:

6.1. Invites emotional expression

“Tell me what feels present for you right now.”

6.2. Mirrors without interpretation

“It sounds like something heavy is here with you.”

6.3. Uses gentle clarifying questions

“What feels strongest right now?”

6.4. Applies soft reflective inquiry (“Why/What”)

“Why do you think this feeling might want your attention today?”

This is not analysis — it is inner orientation.

6.5. Uses the anchor when emotions intensify

“Hold your anchor for a moment — your safe place, your cup of tea, the tree you described.
Notice how it feels to stay with it while the emotion is here.”

7. Therapist Algorithm (Step-by-Step)

Step 1 — Reconnect to Anchor

Begin every Block 2 session by reminding the client of their anchor from Block 1.

Step 2 — Invite Present-Moment Awareness

“Let’s take a moment to notice what is most present for you right now.”

Step 3 — Identify the Emotion

Use soft prompts:

“What emotion feels strongest?”

“If you had to choose one word, what would it be?”

Step 4 — Somatic Mapping

Help the client locate the emotion in the body:
“Where does this emotion live in your body?”

Step 5 — Reflective Clarification

Use “Why?” and “What?” gently:

“Why do you think this feeling might be showing up today?”

“What does this emotion seem to be asking for?”

Step 6 — Emotional Permission

“It’s okay for you to feel this.”

Step 7 — Externalisation (Light)

“If this feeling had a colour or a shape, what would it be?”

Step 8 — Containment

“Imagine placing this feeling into a warm, safe container for a moment.”

Step 9 — Reconnect to Anchor

Ensure stability:
“Return for a moment to your anchor. Tell me when your body feels steadier.”

Step 10 — Assess Readiness for Transition

If:

the emotion is named

the client stabilises

the chaos reduces

→ Transition to Block 3.

If not → continue Block 2 or return to Block 1.

8. Practice Selection Logic for Block 2

Therapist chooses practices based on:

10. Expected Reactions (Normal Responses)

Clients commonly experience:

Relief (“I finally know what I feel”)

Mild sadness

Heaviness that gradually softens

Shifts in body sensations

Slower speech

Tearfulness without flooding

Moments of clarity

11. Red Flags Within Block 2

Stop immediately and return to Block 1 if:

client becomes dissociated

emotional flooding intensifies

client becomes numb, blank, or confused

client begins recalling traumatic scenes

client expresses crisis language

client cannot reconnect to the anchor

symbolic imagery triggers fear

12. Successful Completion Criteria

Block 2 is complete when:

the client can name at least one emotion

the emotion is located somatically

the emotion is acknowledged without panic

the client can articulate why the emotion might be here today
(reflective inquiry)

the system is stable and grounded

the therapist sees predictable regulation

the anchor remains available and effective

Only then can the client safely enter Block 3.

BLOCK 3 — CONTACT WITH THE INNER SELF (ADULT SELF ACTIVATION)

1. Purpose of Block 3

Block 3 establishes a stable, embodied connection with the client’s Inner Adult Self – the inner figure that can:

observe without collapsing into symptoms,

feel without being overwhelmed,

make choices instead of repeating automatic patterns,

hold younger, frightened or hurt parts safely.

This block is the central axis of

MindReset

.
Without an accessible Adult Self, work with parts (Block 4), external beliefs (Block 5) and integration (Block 6) becomes unstable, reactive or potentially unsafe.

The main aims of Block 3 are to help the client:

experience a clear sense of “I” that is more than their pain,

differentiate between “me” and “my emotion/part”,

begin to feel that there is someone inside who can lead, decide and protect.

2. Psychological Methods Used in Block 3

Block 3 uses a light, non-trauma application of:

2.1. Ego-State Therapy (Adult ego activation)

Identifying and reinforcing the Adult ego-state.

Differentiating Adult vs Child vs Protector modes.

2.2. Internal Family Systems (IFS-Informed, light)

Recognising that different “parts” exist.

Accessing Self-energy (Calm, Curiosity, Compassion, Clarity) without deep parts processing.

Introducing the idea that the Adult Self leads, while parts are listened to.

2.3. Mindfulness-Based Self-Observation

“I notice that there is sadness in me” instead of “I am sadness”.

Building capacity for witnessing inner experience.

2.4. Polyvagal-Informed Regulation

Using breath, posture and orientation to support regulated, adult presence.

Moving from hyperarousal or collapse into grounded engagement.

All methods are used in a gentle, present-focused way, with no entry into traumatic scenes.

3. Core Philosophy of Block 3

3.1. No deep work without an Adult Self

MindReset

 assumes that stable inner leadership is required before:

meeting vulnerable child parts,

touching old memories,

changing identity-level patterns.

The Adult Self does not have to be perfect. It only has to be:

a little calmer,

a little wiser,

a little more able to choose than the symptom.

3.2. Inner leadership instead of self-attack

Many clients arrive with an internal critic or harsh “adult” voice.
Block 3 explicitly builds a different kind of inner adult:

firm but kind,

protective without aggression,

realistic but not hopeless.

3.3. Human warmth and anchor remain central

The therapist:

stays warm, steady, emotionally present;

keeps returning to the anchor from Block 1 (garden, blanket, tea, car, tree, etc.);

models adult presence in their tone and pacing.

3.4. Gentle reflective inquiry

The therapist uses “what” and soft “why” questions to support inner orientation:

“What part of you is speaking now?”

“Why might your older

self see

 this differently today?”

This is not analysis of the past, but gentle structuring of the inner world in the present.

4. Indications (When to Use Block 3)

Block 3 is indicated when:

Block 1 and Block 2 criteria are met (basic stabilisation + emotional awareness),

the client can name at least one emotion,

the client tends to fuse with emotions (“I am panic”, “I am a failure”),

the client feels “lost” or “without a self”,

the client is curious about “who I really am” beyond symptoms.

5. Contraindications

Block 3 should not be used when:

the client is in acute crisis: panic, freeze, shutdown → use Block 1;

emotional intensity > 7/10 and not reducible → stabilise first;

the client dissociates when asked to look inside;

any talk of “older self” or “inside” immediately triggers trauma scenes;

psychosis, severe reality distortion, or strong suicidal ideation are present.

In such cases the therapist:

returns to Block 1 (STOP) or keeps the work within Block 2,

and/or considers referral to specialist trauma / psychiatric services.

**6. Depth Model for Block 3

(Surface / Middle / Prohibited Deep)**

6.1. Surface Level — Basic Inner Observation

Focus:

Language shift: “there is anger in me” instead of “I am anger”.

Noticing that there is a part that feels, and a part that notices.

Using anchor whenever intensity rises.

No explicit parts mapping, no imagery of child states.

6.2. Middle Level — Activation of the Inner Adult Self

Focus:

Sensing an older, steadier presence inside.

Noticing how this Adult Self would sit, breathe, speak.

Letting this Adult Self stand next to the current emotion (not above it).

No deep exploration of childhood history, no trauma narrative.

6.3. Deep Level — Prohibited in

MindReset

 Core

Prohibited in Block 3:

intensive inner system mapping,

trauma rescripting,

confrontations between parts,

negotiations with persecutory parts.

Such work belongs to advanced, specialist ego-state/IFS treatment, not to

MindReset

 core protocol.

7. Dialogue Dynamics (Therapist Positioning)

The therapist maintains:

slow, grounded voice;

neutral, compassionate language;

minimal interpretation;

a collaborative, non-

hierarchical



stance

.

Example tone:

“As you feel this sadness, notice if there is also a part of you that can see it from a little distance.”
“If you imagine an older version of yourself — a bit wiser, a bit steadier — where is that in your body right now?”

8. Role of the Anchor in Block 3

The anchor found in Block 1 is now:

a bridge between dysregulated states and Adult Self activation;

a testing ground: “Can my Adult Self exist in this safe place?”;

a resource the client can return to whenever the inner work feels too intense.

Typical moves:

“Let your Adult Self stand with you in your favourite safe place.”

“Feel the blanket / garden / car / tree — and from there, notice the adult presence in you.”

The anchor keeps Block 3 emotionally grounded and body-based, not purely cognitive.

9. Client Types & Typical Presentations

9.1. “No Self” Client

Reports emptiness or confusion: “I don’t know who I am.”

Struggles to feel any inner centre.

Work: micro-sensations, very small steps:
“Notice the part of you that notices you feel empty.”

9.2. Symptom-Fused Client

“I am my anxiety/anger/shame.”

Identity collapsed into symptom.

Work: language shift, observer practices, then Adult Self as “the one who sees and cares”.

9.3. Over-Responsible Client

Externally functioning, internally feels like a scared child “in an adult costume”.

Work: distinguish “performing adult” vs “true inner Adult”; validate exhaustion; introduce kind leadership.

9.4. Detached/Intellectual Client

Avoids feelings, prefers analysis.

Work: start cognitively (“what would your wiser self say?”), then gradually bring in body and anchor.

9.5. Highly Imaginative Client

Easily visualises inner figures.

Work: imagery-based Adult Self, but with clear limits (no trauma scenes, no dramatic family systems).

10. Practice Selection Logic

Therapist chooses practices based on:

10.1. Stability

Unstable → only surface-level observer work + anchor.

Moderately stable → Adult Self activation through posture/breath.

Stable → short, light inner dialogues between Adult Self and current emotion (not child parts yet).

10.2. Client Preference / Typology

Visual → imagery of older self in anchor place.

Cognitive → values-based Adult Self (“the part of me who chooses differently”).

Somatic → Adult Self through posture and breath.

Emotional → Adult Self as a warm, steady inner figure next to the feeling.

12. Therapist Algorithm (Step-by-Step)

Check stabilisation (Block 1 & 2 criteria).

Reconnect to anchor (brief).

Introduce observer position:
“Notice that there is a part of you that can see this feeling.”

Test for Adult Self:

“Imagine an older, steadier you.”

Ask where and how it is sensed (body, image, tone).

Embody Adult Self:

posture adjustment,

slower breath,

tone shifts.

Link to anchor:
Adult Self standing/sitting in the safe place.

Light leadership test:
“What might this adult part say to the feeling you noticed?”

Normalise and reinforce:
“This presence has always been in you; we are simply meeting it more clearly now.”

Monitor for overwhelm:
If intensity rises — back to anchor, back to observer.

Assess completion:

Can client access Adult Self more than once?

Is there a felt difference between “me” and “my feeling/part”?

If yes → prepare for Block 4.

13. Expected Reactions

Healthy reactions:

Relief (“I feel more like myself”),

Clearer thinking,

Slight emotional softening,

New sense of inner support,

Growing curiosity about inner world.

Manageable tension:

Fear of responsibility,

Shame about “not having been adult enough”,

Grief about past decisions.

Therapist responds with validation, not pressure.

14. Red Flags

Stop or step down if:

client shifts into a much younger state and cannot come back;

dissociation increases;

intrusive trauma scenes appear;

client expresses “I do not exist / I am nothing”;

internal voices become hostile, persecutory or chaotic.

Action:

return to Block 1 stabilisation and/or Block 2;

consider specialist referral for complex trauma/dissociation.

15. Completion Criteria

Block 3 is considered complete when the client:

can reliably access an Inner Adult Self (not just once, but reproducibly);

clearly distinguishes “I” (Adult Self) from “my emotion/part”;

feels more coherent, less merged with symptoms;

can hold an emotion while also staying in Adult Self + anchor;

shows readiness and stability to meet inner parts in Block 4.

BLOCK 4 — MEETING THE INNER PARTS & CHILD SELF

4. Block Overview — Meeting the Inner Parts & Child Self

Purpose

To safely and compassionately access the client’s inner emotional parts — including younger self-states, formative emotional fragments, and past unmet needs — in order to restore internal coherence, reduce fragmented self-experience, and prepare the system for deeper integration.

This block reconnects the client with disowned, forgotten, or frozen emotional states, without reliving trauma, and allows the Adult Self to become an anchor for inner safety and re-parenting.

1. Clinical Rationale

Human psychological suffering often arises from:

unmet childhood needs

internalised fear or shame states

early emotional experiences that were never resolved

parts of self that split off for protection

MindReset

 approaches this through non-regressive parts work, where the client’s Adult Self forms a stabilising, compassionate presence.

This block restores:

internal continuity

emotional coherence

access to emotional truth without overwhelm

the ability to self-anchor and self-soothe

2. Indications (When Block 4 Should Be Used)

client reports feeling “fragmented”, “not myself”, “confused inside”

recurring emotional patterns (e.g., shame, collapse, people-pleasing, self-blame)

strong emotional responses without clear cause

moments of “inner child” reactivity

sense of inner conflict or opposing drives

client expresses desire to understand “why I feel like this”

3. Contraindications

Block 4 should NOT be used when:

dissociation is moderate or high

client is in acute crisis or panic

client cannot access the Adult Self at all

psychosis, mania, severe personality structure instability

client begins describing trauma scenes in sensory detail (vision/sound/smell)

emotional flooding is present

there is no grounding anchor available

4. Methods Used in Block 4

The block relies on:

4.1 Trauma-informed parts work

(IFS-informed but non-regressive; no reliving)

4.2 Emotion–body mapping

Accessing parts via sensations, not trauma content.

4.3 Symbolic and non-sensory imagery

Images appear only if the client naturally produces them.

4.4 Anchored Adult Self stabilisation

Adult Self remains the lead figure.

4.5 Gentle imaginative contact

Not forced; adapted for cognitive, emotional, or sensory types.

4.6 Three-tier layering system

Surface → Middle → Deep, with stabilisation between each layer.

5. Therapist Positioning

The therapist must remain:

warm and slow

non-directive

attuned to micro-shifts

neutral but compassionate

stabilising, not analysing

supportive, not interpreting

No internal meaning is imposed — the client discovers their own.

6. Three-Layer System (how depth progresses)

6.1 Surface Layer — Initial Contact

locating a sense of “something inside”

mild emotions: sadness, tension, shyness

first glimmers of younger feelings

the part may appear as sensation, colour, word, or emotion

Work stays very gentle.
If the client stabilises → move to Middle Layer.

6.2 Middle Layer — Emotional Identification

clearer sense of age or emotional tone

part may faintly show needs or fears

client may begin speaking “from” or “about” the part

part may seek recognition or reassurance

If regulated, stable, and anchored → move to Deep Layer.

6.3 Deep Layer — Reconnection & Re-parenting

(This is optional and only for clients who naturally reach it.)

the part expresses unmet need

Adult Self responds with protection, reassurance, presence

symbolic actions may occur (e.g., holding, stepping away from past context, returning something to adult-self, reclaiming self-worth)

no trauma reenactment

no sensory reliving of original events

Only done if:

client is stable

Adult Self is strong

therapist sees no signs of overwhelm

7. Recognition Markers of Inner Parts (NEW section)

7.1 Readiness markers:

slight emotional shift

body softens or focuses

client says “I feel something”

image, sense, emotion, or thought arises

client slows down naturally

7.2 Resistance markers:

part hides (“I see nothing”)

client becomes analytical

jokes, minimises

changes topic

sighs sharply or disconnects

Therapist response → Return to Surface Layer grounding.

7.3 Overwhelm markers:

chest pressure increases

breathing shallow

crying escalates to panic

client dissociates

emotional flashbacks

client says “I can’t be here”

Therapist response → Immediately stop part-work. Go to STOP stabilisation.

7.4 Regression markers (NOT dangerous; just information):

softer voice

simpler sentences

body posture closes

client feels “small”, “tiny”, “young”

Therapist response →
“Your adult self is here with you. Can you feel even 1% of that presence?”

The client can:

access the Adult Self reliably,

stay emotionally regulated most of the session,

return to Adult Self within 10–20 seconds when prompted.

MII-2 — Clear Recognition of Parts

The client can identify at least one younger part through:

emotion,

body sensation,

metaphor,

imagery,

OR cognitive description.

The form does not matter — consistency does.

MII-3 — No Overwhelm During Part Contact

Signs of stability:

the client can look at, feel, or sense the part without panic, detachment, or shutdown,

emotions rise but stay tolerable.

MII-4 — Safe Adult–Part Relationship

The client demonstrates at least one:

compassion,

curiosity,

acceptance,

willingness to comfort.

If there is fear, rejection, disgust, hostility → Block 4 continues.

MII-5 — Basic Reparenting Capacity

The client can offer the part:

a soothing phrase (“You are safe now”),

a gesture (hand on heart),

an intention to protect.

This must come from the Adult Self, not the part.

MII-6 — No Delayed Destabilisation

Within 48 hours after sessions:

no panic,

no dissociation,

no persistent emotional flooding,

normal functioning is preserved.

If destabilisation appears → therapist returns to earlier layers of Block 4.

MII-7 — Internal Cohesion Awareness

The client can verbalise:

“I feel this part inside me,”

or “I know where she/he is,”

or “I feel connected to them.”

Form doesn't matter. Stability does.

BLOCK 5 — BREAKING WITH THE EXTERNAL

Removing Foreign Beliefs, Roles, and Internalised Others

1. Purpose of Block 5

The aim of Block 5 is to help the client safely separate their authentic sense of self from external material absorbed earlier in life, including:

internalised parental voices,

harmful beliefs,

shame-based identity layers,

imposed roles (“the strong one”, “the invisible one”, “the caretaker”),

emotional burdens that never originally belonged to the client.

This block is not about rejecting family or culture.
It is about removing only what damages the client’s wellbeing, while honouring their family values and cultural identity.

Block 5 restores identity cleanliness after parts integration in Block 4, creating space for the true self to emerge.

2. Preconditions (When Block 5 Can Begin)

Block 5 begins only if Block 4 is stably completed.

Therapist must confirm MII —

MindReset

 Integration Index from Block 4:

Required conditions:

Stable Adult Self accessible ≥70% of the session.

The client can recognise younger parts without emotional overwhelm.

The client reports a clear felt sense of internal unity (e.g., “I feel them inside”, “I sense all parts in me”, “I’m not split anymore”).

No emotional destabilisation within the past 48 hours after Block 4 sessions.

The client expresses readiness to examine external influences.

If any requirement is missing → therapist returns to Block 4.

3. Therapeutic Methods Used in Block 5

Block 5 integrates three psychological modalities:

3.1 IFS-Informed Externalisation

Separating “me” from “not-me”.

Identifying burdens and internalised voices not belonging to the client.

3.2 Schema Therapy Micro-Rescripting

Recognising introjected parental or authority voices.

Replacing harsh internal messages with balanced adult-led internal dialogue.

3.3 Gestalt Boundary Work

Establishing clear internal boundaries.

Understanding where external influence ends and the client’s authentic self begins.

These methods are applied softly, symbolically, and in a non-trauma-specific way.

4. The Three-Layer Model of Block 5

Just like Blocks 1–4, Block 5 follows a three-layer system:

4.1 Surface Layer — Gentle Identification

Light recognition of external beliefs.

No confrontation, no deep analysis.

Goal: awareness without destabilisation.

4.2 Middle Layer — Boundary Formation

Clear differentiation between internal and external material.

Symbolic distancing from foreign roles/voices.

Goal: “This is not mine.”

4.3 Deep Layer — Controlled Release

Only accessible if Surface and Middle layers show stability.

Symbolic returning of burdens, stepping out of roles, dissolving introjects.

Goal: internal freedom without emotional overwhelm.

If at any layer the client destabilises → therapist returns to earlier layers or to Block 1/4.

5. Indications (Signs the Client Needs Block 5)

Block 5 is appropriate when the client expresses:

“I feel pressure that isn’t mine.”

“I hear my mother/father in my head.”

“I’m living someone else's life.”

“I always feel guilty even when I’ve done nothing wrong.”

“I must always be good/strong/quiet/perfect.”

6. Contraindications

Block 5 is not allowed if:

The client is emotionally unstable.

Adult Self is not consistently accessible.

Shame spiral is still active.

Parts fear punishment, rejection, or abandonment.

Client expects therapist to decide “what is good or bad”.

If any contraindication appears → therapist returns to Block 4.

7. Adapting Block 5 to Processing Styles

Different clients process information differently.
Therapist adapts the same task to the client's dominant processing mode:

7.1 Visual Processors

See the externalised voice or role as a figure or shape.

Use imagery of distance/space/boundary.

Helpful phrase: “Let’s imagine this belief outside you — how does it look?”

7.2 Somatic Processors

Sense burdens as body tension or pressure.

Work through expansion/relaxation sensations.

Helpful phrase: “Where does your body tighten when this voice appears?”

7.3 Cognitive Processors

Identify foreign beliefs through phrases or verbal patterns.

Use sentence deconstruction and logic.

Helpful phrase: “Whose tone is this? Who talked like this?”

Therapist must match the style to reduce emotional overload.

BLOCK 6 — INTEGRATION & IDENTITY CONSOLIDATION

1. Purpose of the Block

The aim of Block 6 is to integrate the healed parts, reclaimed emotions, updated meaning systems, and de-attached “non-self” material into a stable, coherent, adult identity.
This is the transition from “I survived my past” → to → “I am now living as myself.”

This block forms the core consolidation stage of

MindReset

:

integrating all earlier insights;

stabilising emotional, behavioural and cognitive patterns;

forming the “Adult Self” as the main operating identity;

preventing regression or collapse of the new system;

preparing the client for future identity creation (Block 7).

Integration of Supports & Restoration of the Self

The Formation of the Inner Centre (Adult Self)

In

MindReset

, integration is not limited to gathering previously disconnected emotional parts or resolving past imprints. The deeper aim of this block is the formation of a stable inner centre of navigation — the Adult Self. This centre is not a metaphorical “ideal self” but a felt, lived internal anchor that emerges naturally when previously fragmented experiences have been met, soothed, and reintegrated.

During this process, the client begins to sense a coherent internal organiser:

a steady inner voice that is calm rather than reactive,

a bodily feeling of

groundedness

 or inner solidity,

an ability to choose responses instead of being pulled by old patterns,

a renewed sense of direction, meaning, and personal agency.

This is the moment when the client can “feel themselves from the inside” — not as scattered parts, but as a unified person with a clear centre of gravity. The role of the therapist is to help the client recognise, stabilise, and strengthen this centre so that it becomes a reliable inner support they can return to during future stress.

2. When to Use — Indications

Block 6 is used ONLY when:

Block 4 is fully stabilised (MII Passed)

All relevant inner parts are located, recognised, validated and emotionally regulated.

The client reports a felt sense of internal cohesion (“I can feel them inside me”, “They are with me”, “I am whole”).

No part feels abandoned or in acute distress.

No active re-traumatisation, no inner conflicts requiring immediate work.

Block 5 is completed

Client successfully detached external, parental, societal or internalised material that belonged to others.

Client can differentiate between self-originated and foreign-imposed impulses.

Emotional and behavioural space is opened for identity consolidation.

The client shows readiness for identity-building:

interest in future self-concepts;

stable emotional baseline;

capacity to reflect without spiralling;

desire to change behavioural patterns.

If ANY of the above is missing → return to Blocks 4 or 5.

3. Contraindications

Block 6 is NOT permitted when:

The client cannot yet feel internal parts (MII = fail).

There is internal conflict between parts (“this one hates that one”).

The client still feels dominated by external/parental/internalised voices.

Severe anxiety, dissociation, shutdown, or panic episodes are ongoing.

Client reports “nothing is real”, “I feel like no one”, “I cannot identify myself”.

Identity instability with risk of dissociation.

In such cases → stabilisation + rework of Blocks 3–5 is required.

6. Therapist Algorithm — Step-by-Step

Step 1 — Check MII (

MindReset

 Integration Indicator)

Therapist must confirm:

“Do you feel all your younger parts inside?”

“Do any of them feel scared or left behind?”

“Do you sense yourself as one whole person?”

“Is there any part that still feels separate, angry, unseen?”

If ANY answer indicates fragmentation → return to Block 4.

Step 2 — Establish the Adult Identity Seat

The therapist helps client locate the “Adult Self” through:

body posture

tone of voice

sense of internal spaciousness

ability to comfort inner parts

ability to differentiate “past me” vs “current me”

For non-visual clients — use somatic and cognitive versions.

Step 3 — Unified Parts Alignment

Client brings inner parts “close”, not as forced integration, but:

“Where is your younger part now?”

“How is she/he/they reacting?”

“Do they trust you as the adult?”

“Is there any conflict between them?”

Therapist helps create consensus, not fusion.

Step 4 — Clearing the “Old Identity Residue”

This is NOT trauma work.
This is cognitive-emotional cleaning.

Examples:

“I am always the neglected one”

“I cannot trust myself”

“I am weak”

“I am unsafe alone”

These are addressed through adult reframing and somatic grounding.

Step 5 — Installing the Adult Operating System (AOS)

The client rehearses:

adult voice

adult internal dialogue

adult decisions

adult behavioural micro-shifts

adult boundaries

This creates identity consolidation.

Step 6 — Introduce the Safe Future Self

Without trauma imagery.
It is only a direction, not a “place”.

Prompts:

“What qualities feel most natural for your adult self?”

“What is one behaviour your adult self chooses now?”

“How do your inner parts feel when you imagine this future version?”

Step 7 — Identity Anchoring Ritual

The client chooses:

an object,

a gesture,

a phrase,

a place,

a sensory cue

…which represents their Adult Self.

These anchors were first introduced in Block 1 and now become permanent.

Step 8 — Consolidation Check

Therapist verifies:

emotional stability

inner alignment

absence of distress

acceptance from inner parts

sense of “rightness”

BLOCK 7 — CREATION OF THE NEW IDENTITY MAP

1. Purpose of the Block

Block 7 helps the client shift from restored internal stability (Block 6) to a gentle exploration of their emerging identity.

This block is NOT about external decisions.
It is about:

sensing the quality of the new Self

defining the inner compass

recognising new internal boundaries

discovering what “being me now” feels like

forming the first internal draft of the future, without acting on it yet

The aim is to create a soft, embodied, internal identity map, not a behavioural plan.

2. Role of the Therapist / Clinician

Facilitate inner discovery, not impose meaning.

Stabilise the client’s pace and prevent impulsive conclusions.

Encourage exploration of states, not goals.

Maintain slow tempo — “one breath at a time”.

Contain emotional surges and identity euphoria.

Keep the client in inner experience, not outer reactions.

Gently prevent the client from making drastic decisions.

3. Entry Criteria for Block 7

The client may begin Block 7 only when:

Emotional Stability

baseline affect is stable

no active emotional overwhelm

no acute distress after Block 6 sessions

Internal Integration Status

all major internal parts are acknowledged

internal system feels “together”, even if not perfect

the client can sense their inner centre reliably

the client is not dissociating from parts or emotions

Self-Presence

the client can speak from the Adult Self

the client can witness emotions without collapse

the client maintains a grounded body awareness

MII Check Passed

The

MindReset

 Integration Indicator shows:

internal parts are accessible without conflict

Adult Self remains steady during recall

client reports inner clarity, even mild

no urgent urges to “fix life” externally

4. Contraindications

Block 7 must NOT begin if:

the client is still emotionally volatile

parts continue to compete for dominance

there is a strong impulse to act out (quit job, leave partner, confront family, move house, etc.)

the client expresses suicidal or self-harming ideation

dissociation episodes are present

the client cannot hold the Adult Self for more than a few minutes

the client is still returning to Block 4 content spontaneously

If any of these appear → return to Block 6 or Block 4 depending on cause.

5. Psychological Methods Used in Block 7

Block 7 is built upon:

1. Parts-Informed Identity Consolidation

(IFS-informed, Internal Family Systems)

2. Narrative Reorientation

(Re-authoring, future-self narrative without behavioural push)

3. Embodied Identity Sensing

(Somatic experiencing principles)

4. Values Mapping

(ACT-informed — Acceptance & Commitment Therapy)

5. Mild Imagination-Based Exploration

(Gentle, non-hypnotic visualisations for clients who prefer imagery)

6. Grounding and Regulation

(To prevent identity euphoria or impulsive visions)

All methods remain safe, non-clinical, non-trauma-deepening.

6. Three-Layer Structure of Block 7

Exactly as in Blocks 1–6.

Layer 1 — Surface Exploration

Goal:
Introduce the idea of “new Self” gently, test client stability.

Focus on:

noticing internal changes

naming new qualities (“I feel calmer”, “I have more clarity”)

grounding these sensations

asking small, safe reflective questions

No future-vision work yet.

Layer 2 — Middle Exploration

Goal:
Discover the internal map of the new identity.

Focus on:

sensing how the new Self feels in the body

exploring new internal values (“What matters to me now?”)

defining internal boundaries through imagery or language

identifying what feels aligned / misaligned in current life

Still no behavioural plans, no decisions.

Layer 3 — Deep Exploration

Goal:
Form a symbolic identity map — a stable inner compass.

Focus on:

visual or somatic symbolic representation of identity
(e.g., light, landscape, posture, texture)

defining internal direction, not goals

identifying qualities the new

Self will

 embody

“felt sense of who I am becoming”

Critical safety rule:
No behavioural suggestions. No life decisions. No “You should”.

7. How to Identify If the Client Is Ready for Layer Progression

The client may move deeper ONLY if:

they remain emotionally steady

they do not slip into urgency or impulsivity

they do not start planning life changes

they stay in the Adult Self

their body remains grounded

If impulses arise:
→ return to grounding
→ postpone deeper layers

BLOCK 8 — STABILISATION & EMBODIMENT OF THE NEW IDENTITY

1. Purpose of Block 8

Block 8 consolidates the client’s newly formed identity and transitions it from an internal psychological construct into daily behavioural reality.

The block ensures that:

the new Self becomes stable, embodied, and functionally dominant;

residual automatisms from the old identity are recognised, not feared, and gently retrained;

the client develops a sustainable sense of autonomy,

groundedness

, and internal leadership;

no impulsive life-changing actions are taken during the sensitive stabilisation period;

the new identity becomes the client’s primary operating system in daily life.

MindReset

 considers Block 8 the period where integration turns into embodiment.

2. Entry Criteria

The therapist may initiate Block 8 only when:

Block 6 (Integration of Parts) is clinically complete and the integrated Self is stable.

Block 7 (New Life Line & Internal Narrative) is coherent, emotionally neutral or positive.

All inner parts feel “present inside”, even if their felt-sense varies (

MindReset

 Internal Integration Score ≥ 70%).

Client demonstrates consistent internal orientation (“I know who I am now”).

No acute emotional destabilisation for at least 7–14 days.

No Red Flags from Safety Protocol (Section 2).

If any criterion fails → return to Block 6 or 7.

3. Contraindications

Do not continue to Block 8 if:

client exhibits manic uplift, impulsive planning, grandiosity;

client expresses desire for immediate dramatic life changes (e.g., sudden divorce, resignation, relocation);

signs of depersonalisation, derealisation, or dissociation re-emerge;

somatic panic symptoms significantly increase;

the new identity is not yet emotionally “owned” by the client.

Block 8 must not be started if the integration is fragile.

4. Core Methods Used in Block 8

MindReset

 uses the following clinical frameworks:

Self-Stabilisation Model

Ego-strengthening (analytic tradition)

Stabilisation Practices

Anchoring objects & routines

Safe repetition of new behaviours

Layer-based observation of residual automatisms

5. Expected Emotional & Somatic Reactions

These are normal and expected:

Layer 1 — Surface Layer

mild fatigue, emotional softness

increased intuition or clarity

initial noticing of old habits (“I caught myself doing it again”)

gentle somatic release (warmth, pressure, yawns)

Layer 2 — Middle Layer

oscillation between the new and old identity

moments of grief for the “old self”

temporary disorientation (“I feel different, and it’s strange”)

heightened sensitivity to boundaries and interpersonal dynamics

Layer 3 — Deep Layer

clear sense of internal leadership

strong felt-sense of “I am now myself”

calm decisiveness without emotional spikes

increased resilience to triggers

natural spontaneous behaviours aligned with new identity

If any reaction becomes severe → return to Blocks 5–6.

6. Three-Layer Stabilisation Model (TLSM)

MindReset

 emphasises gradual embodiment:

Layer 1 — Recognition of Old Automatisms
The client notices habits of the old identity without self-shame.
Therapist helps the client name them neutrally.

Layer 2 — Conscious Redirection
The new identity becomes active in moment-to-moment choices:
“What would my new Self do right now?”

Layer 3 — Embodiment
The new identity becomes automatic; the old identity fades.
Behaviour, voice tone, daily decisions, and emotional reactions realign.

Progression is non-linear; loops are expected.

7.6 Stabilisation Grounding

Short grounding routine to maintain emotional neutrality during adjustment.

8. Therapist Algorithm (Clinical Step-by-Step)

Step 1 — Confirm Full Eligibility

Use Block 6 & 7 checklists and MIIS (

MindReset

 Integration Indicator Scale).

Step 2 — Explain Purpose

Therapist explains that Block 8 is consolidation, not transformation.

Step 3 — Introduce Conscious Observation

Client learns to notice old automatisms without judgement.

Step 4 — Establish the Identity Anchor

Anchor chosen in Block 1 is now used daily.

Step 5 — Map Identity-Aligned Behaviours

Therapist identifies small low-risk behaviours to reinforce the new Self.

Step 6 — Stabilise Boundaries

Help the client recognise new interpersonal behaviours without overcorrection.

Step 7 — Prevent Impulsive Life Rebuilds

Therapist explicitly instructs:
“No major life decisions for now — observe first.”

Step 8 — Track Progress with TLSM

Therapist checks which layer the client is currently embodying.

Step 9 — Address Setbacks

If the client falls into old patterns — treat it as neutral information, not regression.

Step 10 — Prepare for Discharge Protocol

When identity is stable ≥ 4–6 weeks, begin transition to maintenance.

9. Risk Management

Therapist must prevent:

impulsive breakups, resignations, relocations

sudden financial or legal actions

confrontations with abusers

attempts to “prove” the new identity through risky behaviour

emotional over-excitement that looks like breakthrough but is actually instability

Clients must remain in low-risk behavioural territory until identity stabilises.

10. Clinical Timeline Expectations

Typical stabilisation:

Weeks 1–4: oscillation between new/old reactions

Weeks 4–12: identity begins to dominate

3–6 months: stable behavioural embodiment

6–12 months: full integration into life roles and relationships

(These ranges vary by personality type and trauma history.)

11. Red Flags

Immediate pause and return to earlier blocks if:

client urges to make sudden life-changing decisions

new identity feels “empty”, “fake”, or “not mine”

intrusive memories reappear

dissociation, depersonalisation, panic

internal parts feel “lost” or “gone”

somatic overwhelm increases

sleep disruption becomes severe

12. Therapist Checklist

Therapist verifies:

identity anchor is used without resistance

new Self is emotionally “owned”

automatisms are observed consciously

behaviours begin aligning naturally

no impulsive plans

no destabilisation

TLSM indicates stable progression

client shows self-directedness and autonomy

inner emotional tone is neutral or positive

13. Discharge Protocol

A client may complete Block 8 when:

New identity is stable ≥ 6–12 weeks.

Automatisms are caught early and redirected easily.

Behavioural patterns shift naturally without stress.

Anchor works reliably.

Client expresses:
“I feel like myself, and I understand how to live from here.”

Client receives Stabilisation After-Care Sheet:

Includes:

expected mild fluctuations

grounding routines

identity reinforcement prompts

instructions for setbacks

immediate steps if overwhelm returns

guidance on delaying major life decisions until full stability

Follow-up sessions recommended at: 2 weeks → 1 month → 3 months → 6 months.
